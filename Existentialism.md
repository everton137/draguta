# Existentialism

**Existentialism** is a philosophical movement, emerging principally in 19th and 20th century Europe, that places **individual existence**, **freedom**, **choice**, and **authenticity** at the center of philosophical inquiry. Its core claim: existence precedes essence — humans are not born with a fixed nature or purpose; they must create their own meaning.

## Core Themes

### Existence Precedes Essence
The defining formula of Jean-Paul Sartre: unlike a hammer (whose essence — what it is for — precedes its manufacture), humans have no pre-given purpose or essence. We exist first and define ourselves through choice and action.

This is the radical implication of [[Nietzsche]]'s "death of God": if there is no God and no natural order, there is no given human nature or purpose. Freedom is absolute — and terrifying.

### Radical Freedom and Responsibility
Every person is **radically free** — condemned, as Sartre put it, to be free. Even choosing not to choose is a choice. With this freedom comes absolute responsibility: you cannot blame God, society, or human nature for who you are. You are your choices.

This connects to [[Free Will]] — existentialists generally affirm a robust sense of human freedom against determinism.

### Authenticity
To live **authentically** is to own one's freedom, acknowledge one's facticity (the situation you are thrown into), and make genuine choices.

**Bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*) is the self-deception of pretending you have no freedom — playing a role, following conventions, hiding behind "everyone does this." The waiter who *is* a waiter through and through, rather than playing the role, is Sartre's famous example.

### Anxiety (Angst)
Genuine awareness of freedom produces **existential anxiety** — the vertigo of having no fixed point, no given essence. [[Heidegger]] called *Angst* the mood that reveals the fundamental structure of existence (*Dasein*).

### Thrownness and Facticity
We do not choose where, when, or to whom we are born — our **thrownness** is the brute contingency of our situation. Existence is the project of what we make of what we have been thrown into.

### Death
Heidegger argued that **being-toward-death** is central to authentic existence. Only by confronting our mortality honestly can we own our lives fully. Avoiding the thought of death leads to inauthenticity.

## Key Figures

### Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855)
The proto-existentialist. Against [[Hegel]]'s system (which dissolves the individual in the Absolute), Kierkegaard insisted on **subjective truth** — "truth is subjectivity." The three stages of existence: aesthetic, ethical, religious. The **leap of faith** as the resolution of despair.

### Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)
See [[Nietzsche]]. The death of God, the Overman, authenticity as self-creation, will to power — all central existentialist themes.

### Martin Heidegger (1889–1976)
*Being and Time* (1927) is the most systematic existentialist work. Key concepts:
- **Dasein** ("being there") — the distinctive mode of human being
- **Being-in-the-world** — we are always already embedded in a world of concerns; the Cartesian isolated subject is an abstraction
- **Thrownness** — always already in a situation not of our choosing
- **Authenticity** vs. **fallenness** into *das Man* ("the they" — the anonymous social norm)
- **Being-toward-death** — confronting mortality as the path to authentic existence

Heidegger drew on [[Phenomenology]] (Husserl's method) and was influenced by [[Hegel]]'s account of historical situatedness. His membership of the Nazi party from 1933 remains profoundly controversial.

### Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980)
France's most famous existentialist. *Being and Nothingness* (1943) develops a radical philosophy of consciousness and freedom. His lecture "Existentialism is a Humanism" (1945) popularized the movement. Later work (*Critique of Dialectical Reason*) attempted to synthesize existentialism with [[Marx]]'s social theory.

### Simone de Beauvoir (1908–1986)
Extended existentialism to feminism in *The Second Sex* (1949): woman has been defined as "Other" — a case of imposed inauthenticity. Women must claim their own freedom. De Beauvoir developed existentialist [[Ethics]] more systematically than Sartre.

### Albert Camus (1913–1960)
Associated with existentialism but preferred "absurdism": life has no meaning, but we must not conclude suicide or irrational belief. We must **rebel** against the absurdity, affirm life anyway — "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

## Existentialism and Other Philosophies

- [[Phenomenology]] — existentialism uses the phenomenological method (Heidegger, Sartre) but turns it toward concrete human existence
- [[Nietzsche]] — the deepest philosophical forerunner
- [[Hegel]] — existentialists reacted against Hegelian system-building and the absorption of the individual in the Absolute
- [[Marx]] — Sartre's later work tried to reconcile existentialism with Marxist social analysis
- [[Ethics]] — existentialist ethics: authenticity, responsibility, the ethics of the Other
- [[Free Will]] — existentialism is one of the most robust defences of radical human freedom
- [[Consciousness]] — existentialism is deeply concerned with the structure of [[Consciousness|conscious]] experience
